Canine Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

URL: https://parthchaudhari.com/bridge/case-studies/dka-dog.html

← Back to Study Games

Signalment and Presenting Complaint

  • BreedLabrador Retriever
  • Age9 years
  • SexFemale Spayed
  • Weight28 kg
  • Presenting ComplaintLethargy, vomiting, polyuria/polydipsia, weight loss

History

The owner reports 10 days of progressive lethargy, reduced appetite, and intermittent vomiting. Over the last month, water intake and urination have increased substantially, and body condition has visibly declined.

The patient has no previous endocrine diagnosis but had recurrent urinary tract infections in the past year. On the morning of presentation, she was weak, reluctant to stand, and refused food entirely.

Physical Exam Findings

  • Tachycardia at 156 bpm with weak peripheral pulses
  • Estimated 8% dehydration with tacky mucous membranes
  • Acetone-like odor on breath
  • Mild tachypnea and depressed mentation
  • Point-of-care blood glucose markedly elevated

Problem List

  1. Severe hyperglycemia with suspected ketosis
  2. Volume depletion and electrolyte derangement risk
  3. Ongoing gastrointestinal fluid loss from vomiting
  4. Potential acid-base disturbance consistent with ketoacidosis

Differentials (Ranked)

  1. Diabetic ketoacidosis
  2. Hyperosmolar hyperglycemic syndrome
  3. Acute pancreatitis with stress hyperglycemia

Treatment Overview

Initial treatment focuses on isotonic fluid resuscitation, electrolyte correction, and then controlled insulin therapy. Serial monitoring of glucose, electrolytes, hydration, and acid-base status guides safe progression through stabilization.

Key Learning Points

  • DKA management starts with perfusion restoration, not immediate insulin bolus.
  • Electrolyte trends can change rapidly once insulin therapy begins.
  • Concurrent triggers (infection, pancreatitis) should be investigated early.
  • Structured monitoring is essential to avoid iatrogenic complications.
